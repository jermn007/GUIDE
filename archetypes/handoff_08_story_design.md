## Overview

This archetype evaluates the quality of narrative-driven instruction, scenario-based learning, case studies, and branching scenarios. It assesses whether stories are structurally sound, whether learning objectives are naturally integrated into the narrative rather than artificially overlaid, and whether the story creates the conditions for authentic transfer of learning to learners' real-world contexts.

**Key insight:** The best story-driven instruction makes it impossible to separate the learning from the narrative. The story *is* the instruction.

---

## Evaluation Dimensions

### Dimension 1: Narrative Structure (1-5)

**Question:** Does the story follow a recognized structural framework?

| Score | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **5** | Clear, identifiable story structure (Story Circle, Hero's Journey, or similar recognized framework). All story phases are well-developed: beginning clearly establishes context, middle builds tension with purpose, end resolves with meaning. Story arc is coherent and feels inevitable in hindsight. |
| **4** | Strong narrative structure with minor gaps. Most phases are present and developed; one phase may be slightly underdeveloped (e.g., mentor phase is brief but adequate). Overall arc is clear and satisfying. |
| **3** | Moderate structure. Some recognizable elements (e.g., setup and climax) but structure is loose or one major phase is missing/weak. Story is coherent but feels somewhat episodic. |
| **2** | Weak or unclear structure. Story elements are present but the overall arc is hard to follow. May feel disjointed or lack clear resolution. |
| **1** | No recognizable structure. Story feels random, fragmented, or lacks narrative coherence. Difficult to identify beginning, middle, end. |

**Frameworks to evaluate against:**
- **Story Circle (Pixar):** Once upon a time → Every day → One day → Because of that → Until finally → Ever since then → Moral
- **Hero's Journey (Campbell):** Ordinary world → Call to adventure → Refusal of the call → Meeting the mentor → Crossing the threshold → Tests and allies → Ordeal → Reward → Road back → Return with the elixir
- **Beat Sheet (Snyder, 2005):** Opening Image → Theme Stated → Set-Up → Catalyst → Debate → Break into Two → B Story → Midpoint → Bad Guys Close In → All Is Lost → Dark Night of the Soul → Break into Three → Finale → Final Image. Snyder's framework maps emotional beats to structural milestones and is especially useful for tightly paced instructional narratives.

**Why it matters:** Structure creates cognitive scaffolding. Learners can follow a well-structured narrative more easily and retain the embedded learning more effectively. Structure also makes the story memorable, which aids recall and transfer.

---

### Dimension 2: Learning-Narrative Integration (1-5)

**Question:** Are learning objectives embedded naturally within the narrative, or artificially overlaid?

| Score | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **5** | Learning objectives are woven into the story's narrative tension. The story tension *is* the learning challenge. No artificial "here's the lesson" moments; learners discover the learning through the story. Instruction never interrupts the narrative flow. Impossible to separate learning from story. |
| **4** | Mostly integrated. Learning objectives emerge from narrative context. Minimal moments where instruction feels overlaid (e.g., one character briefly summarizes a principle, then the story continues). |
| **3** | Mixed integration. Some objectives are naturally embedded; others feel like instruction that the story illustrates. Story and learning coexist but occasionally feel separate. |
| **2** | Instruction dominates. Story serves primarily to illustrate pre-stated learning objectives. Frequent moments where narrative pauses for explanation. Learning feels overlaid on the story. |
| **1** | Story and instruction are disconnected. Learning objectives are external to the narrative; story is window dressing. Instruction regularly interrupts narrative flow. |

**Why it matters:** When learning is integrated into narrative, learners engage more deeply and retain learning longer. When learning is bolted on, engagement drops and transfer becomes less likely.

---

### Dimension 3: Character & Situation Authenticity (1-5)

**Question:** Are characters relatable to the target audience? Are scenarios realistic and relevant?

| Score | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **5** | Characters are vivid, multi-dimensional, and relatable to the target audience. Their motivations are clear and believable. Scenarios are grounded in the learner's actual work/life context. Characters face genuine dilemmas (not obvious right/wrong choices). Situations feel like they could actually happen. |
| **4** | Characters are relatable with minor stereotyping or one-dimensionality. Scenarios are recognizable and mostly realistic. Decisions mostly feel authentic with occasional moments of contrivance. |
| **3** | Characters are adequate but somewhat generic. Scenarios are idealized versions of learner reality. Some decisions feel a bit contrived or lack stakes. Recognizable but not deeply authentic. |
| **2** | Characters feel generic or only partially relatable. Scenarios are somewhat unrealistic or clichéd. Decisions lack authenticity; feel forced or overly simplified. |
| **1** | Characters are unrelatable or stereotyped. Scenarios are unrealistic or divorced from learner context. Decisions are obvious or lack stakes. |

**Why it matters:** Authentic characters and situations increase engagement and likelihood of transfer. Learners see themselves in the characters and situations, making the learning personally relevant. Generic characters reduce emotional investment and transfer potential.

---

### Dimension 4: Emotional Engagement & Motivation (1-5)

**Question:** Does the narrative create emotional stakes that drive engagement without causing anxiety that impairs learning?

| Score | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **5** | Story creates genuine emotional engagement throughout. Productive tension (conflict, mystery, curiosity, meaningful stakes) is present and serves learning. Emotional arcs amplify learning; no anxiety that blocks processing. Learners are motivated to understand how the story resolves. |
| **4** | Strong emotional engagement with mostly productive tension. Minor moments where emotional intensity might be high for some learners but generally supportive of learning. |
| **3** | Moderate emotional engagement. Some tension and stakes present. Mostly productive but occasional moments that could overwhelm learners or cause counterproductive anxiety. |
| **2** | Low emotional engagement or counterproductive anxiety. Tension may be present but doesn't motivate deeper learning or creates worry that impairs processing. Story feels flat or threatening. |
| **1** | Flat affect or overwhelming anxiety. Story fails to engage emotionally or creates anxiety that blocks learning. |

**Why it matters:** Emotional engagement drives deeper cognitive processing and memory consolidation. Productive struggle (not debilitating anxiety) enhances learning. Stories with authentic emotional stakes are more memorable and transferable.

---

### Dimension 5: Decision Points & Branching Quality (1-5)

**Question:** (For interactive/branching scenarios) Are decision points meaningful with distinct, logical consequences?

| Score | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **5** | Decision points are abundant and meaningful. Each choice has distinct, logical consequences that flow naturally from the learner's decision. Wrong paths are instructive (teach something valuable, not just "try again"). Branches diverge significantly-different choices lead to substantively different story paths and learning experiences. |
| **4** | Most decision points are meaningful with clear consequences. A few choices might have overlapping outcomes, but generally branches diverge. Wrong paths teach; mostly avoid arbitrary feedback. |
| **3** | Some meaningful decisions; others feel forced or have overlapping consequences. Branching exists but branches may partially reconverge. Some wrong paths are instructive; others are dismissive. |
| **2** | Few meaningful decisions. Consequences feel forced or arbitrary. Branches reconverge quickly; little substantive divergence. Wrong paths are mostly punitive ("try again") not instructive. |
| **1** | No real decisions (linear story only, or all choices lead to same outcome). Consequences are random or absent. |

**Note:** For linear scenarios without branching, score based on narrative tension and decision-making embedded in the case study itself. For example, in a case study, do characters face meaningful decisions? Are the consequences of their choices clear?

**Why it matters:** Meaningful choices increase learner agency and metacognitive reflection. Learning from the consequences of decisions-both good and bad-enhances understanding and transfer. Branching that reconverges quickly reduces the impact of choices.

---

### Dimension 6: Transfer & Generalizability (1-5)

**Question:** Can learners extract principles from the story that apply to their own situations?

| Score | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **5** | Clear, generalizable principles emerge from the story. Story is specific enough to be engaging and emotionally resonant but open enough for learners to see how principles apply to different contexts. Narrative explicitly or implicitly supports metacognitive reflection ("What would you do in this situation?"). Transfer is likely. |
| **4** | Mostly transferable principles. Story is moderately specific. Learners can see connections to their own contexts, though transfer requires some scaffolding. |
| **3** | Some transferable principles. Story may be somewhat context-specific. Learners can extract *some* learning that applies broadly, but transfer is not guaranteed. |
| **2** | Low transferability. Story is highly specific to one context or industry. Principles are narrow or unclear. Learners may struggle to apply learning to their own situations. |
| **1** | Non-transferable. Story is so specific or situation-dependent that learning doesn't generalize. Learners see no connection to their own contexts. |

**Why it matters:** Transfer is the ultimate goal of instruction. Stories that support transfer are more valuable long-term. Narratives that embed generalizable principles within specific, engaging contexts achieve the best of both worlds.

## Implementation Notes

### Before Evaluation
- Clarify **target audience** context (role, industry, experience level) to assess character/situation authenticity and transfer likelihood
- Specify whether the artifact is **linear** (case study) or **branching** (interactive scenario) to calibrate Decision Points scoring
- Identify stated **learning objectives** to evaluate Learning-Narrative Integration
- Note the **instructional context** (e.g., online course, live workshop, blended) as this affects narrative design constraints

### Interpreting Results

**High overall score (4.5-5.0):** Story-driven instruction is likely to engage learners deeply and support transfer. Consider for validation studies or as a model for other scenarios in your curriculum.

**Mid-range (3.0-4.4):** Instruction is functional but has opportunities for improvement. Check severity flags and improvement suggestions for quick wins. Often, improving Learning-Narrative Integration or Transfer & Generalizability can lift the entire score.

**Lower score (1.0-2.9):** Significant gaps. Consider whether the narrative structure supports instruction at all. May need redesign rather than revision.

### Common Pitfalls to Flag

1. **Story as illustration, not instruction**: Learning objectives are stated up front; story illustrates them. *Fix:* Embed learning objectives in story tension.
2. **Branching that reconverges too quickly**: Learner choice at decision point A, but all paths lead to decision point B. *Fix:* Allow consequences to persist; let branches stay diverged.
3. **Wrong paths are punitive**: "That's incorrect. Try again." *Fix:* Make wrong paths instructive. Show consequences of the choice within the story.
4. **Characters unrelatable to target audience**: Idealized professionals, generic roles, outdated cultural references. *Fix:* Ground characters in actual learner personas and current contexts.
5. **No clear transfer mechanism**: Story is highly specific; learners can't extract generalizable principles. *Fix:* Add metacognitive reflection or ensure story is general enough to be applicable.
