# Archetype 4: Multimedia Content Design (Deep Mayer)
## Handoff Documentation

**Purpose**: Evaluate e-learning modules, videos, interactive media, and slide-based courses for cognitive load optimization and multimedia learning effectiveness.

**Use Cases**:
- Video course review before launch
- Interactive module assessment
- Slide deck validation
- LMS content quality audit
- Accessibility compliance check

---

## Evaluation Dimensions

| Dimension | Scale | Definition | Key Criteria |
|-----------|-------|------------|--------------|
| **Multimedia Principle Compliance** | 1-5 | Does content use words AND graphics, not words alone? | Instructional visuals integrated with text/narration; graphics support learning, not decoration |
| **Extraneous Load Reduction** | 1-5 | Are all five cognitive load principles applied? | Coherence (no seductive details), Redundancy (no duplicate on-screen text + narration), Signaling (organizational cues), Spatial Contiguity (labels near graphics), Temporal Contiguity (related elements simultaneous) |
| **Intrinsic Load Management** | 1-5 | Is complex content segmented and modality-optimized? | Content chunked for learner pacing; pre-training present; modality matched to complexity (audio + graphics for complex; text for simple) |
| **Generative Processing Support** | 1-5 | Does content encourage active cognitive engagement? | Personalization (conversational tone), Voice (human narration preferred), Embodiment (genuine engagement), Image Principle (no distracting presenter images) |
| **Interactivity & Learner Control** | 1-5 | Is interaction meaningful and objective-aligned? | Beyond click-next; learner can control pacing; interactions target learning objectives |
| **Visual Design & Information Architecture** | 1-5 | Is layout clear, accessible, and readable? | Visual hierarchy, whitespace, purposeful color, readable fonts, intuitive navigation |

## Scoring Guide

### Multimedia Principle Compliance
- **5**: Instructional graphics integrated throughout; words and pictures work together; no unnecessary decoration
- **4**: Good use of visuals; mostly instructional; 1-2 decorative elements
- **3**: Mix of instructional and decorative graphics; somewhat integrated
- **2**: Limited instructional graphics; some decorative images; primarily text-based
- **1**: Primarily text-based or only decorative images; no instructional graphics

**What Counts as Instructional**:
- Diagram showing a process (e.g., photosynthesis pathway with arrows and labels)
- Graph displaying data (supported by narration or caption)
- Animation illustrating a concept (e.g., DNA replication)
- Screenshot or example aligned to learning objective

**What's Decorative** (extraneous):
- Stock photo of a happy person at a desk
- Clipart that adds no learning value
- Animated transition effects with no content value

### Extraneous Load Reduction
- **5**: All five principles evident; content is focused and efficient; no seductive details
- **4**: Four principles evident; one minor violation
- **3**: Three to four principles present; one to two violations
- **2**: Two to three principles present; multiple violations
- **1**: Few principles; multiple violations; high extraneous load

**Principle Checklist**:
1. **Coherence**: Is the content focused on learning objective? Are there interesting-but-irrelevant tangents? (E.g., A video on photosynthesis shouldn't spend 2 minutes on the history of photosynthesis research unless objective requires it.)
2. **Redundancy**: If narrating a complex diagram, is the same text also on screen? (For complex content, narration + visual is optimal; adding on-screen text creates redundancy and cognitive overload.)
3. **Signaling**: Are attention-guiding cues present? (Arrows, highlights, labels, zoom-in effects that focus learner attention where it matters.)
4. **Spatial Contiguity**: Are labels near their corresponding graphics? (Don't put a diagram on the left and label definitions on the right; keep label and graphic adjacent.)
5. **Temporal Contiguity**: Are related elements presented at the same time? (Don't show a diagram, discuss it later; show and explain simultaneously.)

### Intrinsic Load Management
- **5**: Content is chunked into learner-paced segments; pre-training evident; modality optimized for complexity
- **4**: Mostly chunked; some pre-training; generally good modality
- **3**: Partially chunked; pre-training implied; mixed modality
- **2**: Long chunks; little pre-training; poor modality choices
- **1**: Unsegmented, dense content; no pre-training; all-text or all-visual

**Segmenting Examples**:
- Video is 3-5 minutes with pause/practice checkpoint
- Slide deck has 1-2 ideas per slide, not 7-8
- Module is broken into 15-minute lessons with break points
- Interactive simulation lets learner explore at own pace

**Pre-Training**:
- Vocabulary introduced before complex narrative
- Simple example shown before complex problem
- Key concepts reviewed before applying them

**Modality Matching**:
- Simple factual content: Text + static image is fine
- Complex procedural content: Narrated animation + graphics (not on-screen text)
- Example: "Teach cell division with narrated animation, not with a wall of text bullet points"

### Generative Processing Support
- **5**: Personalized conversational tone ("you"), human voice, genuine engagement (no distracting presenter images)
- **4**: Mostly personalized; human voice or good synthesis; adequate engagement
- **3**: Some personalization; mixed voice quality; adequate engagement
- **2**: Formal tone; poor voice; minimal engagement cues
- **1**: Highly formal, robotic voice, or very distracting presenter

**Personalization Examples** (Score 5):
- "You're about to learn how to analyze data. Here's why it matters to you..."
- "Let's walk through this example together."

**Formal Tone** (Lower score):
- "The learner will understand the concept of..."
- "This module introduces the principles of..."

**Voice**:
- Human voice (professional, warm, clear) = Higher score
- Synthesized speech (computer-generated) = Lower score (unless high-quality synthesis)
- Mismatched or unprofessional narration = Low score

**Embodiment & Image Principle**:
- Good: Animations, engaged narrator (brief appearance, focused on content)
- Poor: Distracting talking head throughout video; excessive presenter image; unprofessional appearance

### Interactivity & Learner Control
- **5**: Meaningful interactions (branching, simulations, problem-solving, knowledge checks); full learner control (pause, rewind, speed); aligned to objectives
- **4**: Mostly meaningful interactions; good control; well-aligned
- **3**: Some interactions (quizzes, reveal activities); basic control; generally aligned
- **2**: Limited interactions (mostly click-next); limited control
- **1**: No interaction; no learner control; misaligned to objectives

**Meaningful Interaction Examples**:
- Interactive simulation where learner adjusts variables and sees consequences
- Branching scenario with different outcomes based on learner choice
- Knowledge check quiz with corrective feedback
- Drag-and-drop activity where learner must apply knowledge
- Decision-making task aligned to objective

**Shallow Interaction** (Lower score):
- Click-to-reveal (just reveals text without requiring thinking)
- Generic "click next" navigation
- Unrelated games or activities ("click to find the hidden number")

**Learner Control**:
- Play/pause, rewind, speed controls for video
- Ability to navigate non-linearly (go back, skip forward)
- Option to adjust difficulty or branching path

### Visual Design & Information Architecture
- **5**: Clear visual hierarchy, good whitespace, purposeful color, readable fonts, intuitive navigation
- **4**: Mostly clear; minor issues with readability or organization
- **3**: Generally acceptable; some design or navigation issues
- **2**: Poor hierarchy or cluttered; readability issues; confusing layout
- **1**: Highly cluttered, poor contrast, illegible text, chaotic navigation

**Visual Hierarchy**:
- Important information is prominent (larger, bolder, top of page)
- Less important information is secondary
- Use of color, size, and position to guide attention

**Whitespace** (Breathing Room):
- Not crowded; margins and padding between elements
- Text is broken into chunks, not walls of paragraphs
- Don't force 10 slides' worth of content into 1 slide

**Color**:
- Purposeful (supports comprehension or accessibility)
- High contrast between text and background (WCAG AA: 4.5:1 for normal text)
- Not excessive or arbitrary (e.g., every word in a different color)
- Colorblind-friendly (don't rely on color alone to convey meaning)

**Typography**:
- Readable font (sans-serif for screens: Arial, Verdana, Calibri)
- Appropriate size (minimum 14pt for body text on screens)
- Good contrast (dark text on light background preferred)
- Consistent spacing and line height

**Navigation**:
- Clear menu or structure
- Easy to find where you are in the course
- Logical flow (don't jump around without purpose)

## System Prompt (Judge Rubric)

```
You are an expert instructional design evaluator specializing in multimedia learning and cognitive load.

You assess e-learning modules, videos, interactive media, and slide-based courses using six dimensions:

1. **Multimedia Principle Compliance (1-5)**: Does content use words AND graphics, not words alone or graphics alone?
   - Mayer: "people learn better from words and pictures than from words alone"
   - Evaluate: Are visuals instructional (supporting learning) or decorative (distracting)?
   - Score 5: Graphics are instructional and integrated with text/narration; good use of visuals throughout
   - Score 3: Mix of visual and text; some graphics are decorative
   - Score 1: Primarily text-based or only decorative images; missing instructional graphics

2. **Extraneous Load Reduction (1-5)**: Are all five cognitive load principles applied?
   - Coherence: No seductive details (interesting but irrelevant information)
   - Redundancy: On-screen text doesn't duplicate narration for complex content
   - Signaling: Organizational cues (arrows, highlights, labels) guide attention
   - Spatial Contiguity: Labels are near corresponding graphics (not separated)
   - Temporal Contiguity: Related elements are presented at the same time (not separated)
   - Score 5: All five principles evident; content is focused and efficient
   - Score 3: Most principles present; 1-2 minor violations
   - Score 1: Multiple violations; seductive details, redundancy, poor layout

3. **Intrinsic Load Management (1-5)**: Is complex content segmented and modality-optimized?
   - Segmenting: Content broken into learner-paced chunks
   - Pre-Training: Key concepts introduced before complex processes
   - Modality: Audio narration + graphics for complex content (not text + graphics)
   - Score 5: Content is chunked; modality optimized; pre-training evident
   - Score 3: Some segmenting; modality mixed; pre-training implied
   - Score 1: Long, unsegmented content; poor modality choices

4. **Generative Processing Support (1-5)**: Does content encourage active engagement?
   - Personalization: Conversational tone ("you") vs. formal ("the learner")
   - Voice: Human narration (preferred over synthesized)
   - Embodiment: Genuine engagement cues (presenter, animations)
   - Image Principle: No distracting speaker images; focus on content
   - Score 5: Personalized tone, human voice, engaging (no distracting presenter)
   - Score 3: Some personalization; mixed voice quality; adequate engagement
   - Score 1: Formal tone, poor voice, distracting presenter images

5. **Interactivity & Learner Control (1-5)**: Is interaction meaningful and objective-aligned?
   - Is interaction more than "click next"? (e.g., branching, decision-making, knowledge checks)
   - Can learners control pacing (pause, rewind, speed)?
   - Do interactions target learning objectives, not just engagement?
   - Score 5: Meaningful interactions (branching, simulations, decision tasks); full learner control; aligned to objectives
   - Score 3: Some interaction (quizzes, click-to-reveal); basic pacing control; mostly aligned
   - Score 1: Minimal interaction (mostly click-next); no pacing control; unclear alignment to objectives

6. **Visual Design & Information Architecture (1-5)**: Is layout clear, accessible, and readable?
   - Visual hierarchy: Important elements are prominent
   - Whitespace: Breathing room; not cluttered
   - Color: Supports comprehension (not excessive or arbitrary)
   - Typography: Readable font, appropriate size, good contrast
   - Information architecture: Logical organization; easy navigation
   - Score 5: Clear hierarchy, good whitespace, purposeful color, readable fonts, intuitive navigation
   - Score 3: Generally clear; minor readability or organization issues
   - Score 1: Cluttered, poor contrast, confusing layout

Return JSON with overall score (mean of 6 dimensions), individual dimension scores, rationale per dimension, severity_flags (critical issues), and improvement_suggestions (actionable next steps).
```

## Severity Flags (Red Flags)

Look for and flag critical issues:

1. **No Instructional Graphics**: Content is text-only or only decorative images
2. **Extreme Redundancy**: On-screen text fully duplicates complex narration
3. **Wall of Text**: Slides or content blocks are dense, cramped paragraphs
4. **Distracting Presenter**: Talking head fills screen; distracts from content
5. **No Learner Control**: Video cannot be paused, rewound, or controlled
6. **Meaningless Interaction**: "Click to reveal" or unrelated games; no learning value
7. **Poor Accessibility**: Illegible text, low contrast, no captions, no transcripts
8. **Cognitive Overload**: Content is dense, unsegmented, and presented all at once
9. **Misaligned Interaction**: Interactive activities don't target stated objectives

---

## Improvement Suggestions

When scoring < 4 on any dimension, recommend specific next steps:

1. **Multimedia Principle**: Add instructional graphics; replace decorative images with diagrams, animations, or infographics that support learning
2. **Extraneous Load**: Remove seductive details; reduce on-screen text for complex narration; add signaling cues (arrows, highlights); adjust layout for spatial contiguity
3. **Intrinsic Load**: Segment long videos (break into 3-5 min chunks); pre-teach vocabulary; switch to narrated animation for complex processes instead of on-screen text
4. **Generative Processing**: Use conversational language ("you"); switch to human voice; reduce presenter image; add engagement cues
5. **Interactivity**: Replace click-next with branching, simulations, or problem-solving tasks; add learner controls (pause, rewind, speed); ensure interactions align to objectives
6. **Visual Design**: Improve hierarchy (bold key terms, use size/color strategically); increase whitespace; ensure text contrast meets WCAG AA (4.5:1); use readable fonts; clarify navigation

## Input Format

The archetype expects:

- **Input**: Learning objective(s), learner profile, and content context (e.g., "E-learning module on 'Excel Pivot Tables' for business professionals; self-paced; video + interactive simulation")
- **Output**: Screenshots, video descriptions, slide deck, or detailed content outline describing visuals, interactions, narration, and layout
- **Context** (optional): Platform (Articulate Storyline, Moodle, YouTube, Captivate), target audience, accessibility requirements
- **Reference** (optional): Exemplary e-learning module, video, or design guideline for comparison

Example Input/Output:
```
INPUT: "E-learning module on 'Customer Service Call Handling' for call center representatives; learners are adults with varied tech skills; asynchronous, mobile-accessible"

OUTPUT DESCRIPTION:
Module Structure:
- Slide 1: Title slide with learning objectives
- Slide 2-3: Video (4 min) showing a good vs. bad customer service call (narrated; shows examples)
  * Good call: Representative listens, empathizes, solves
  * Bad call: Representative interrupts, dismisses concern
  * Narration: "You'll learn the three keys to great service..."
  * Graphics: Call transcript on-screen (during narration) + visual indicators (checkmark for good, X for bad)
- Slide 4: Interactive branching scenario
  * Learner reads a customer complaint; chooses response (A, B, or C)
  * Each choice leads to different outcome video
  * Feedback explains why choice was good/poor
- Slide 5: Practice quiz (5 questions, knowledge check)
- Slide 6: Real-world application (template for handling similar call)

Design Notes:
- Font: Calibri, 16pt body, 24pt headings
- Colors: Company blue (primary), gray (secondary), white background
- Visuals: Call transcript, branching scenario decision trees, video demonstrations
- Interaction: Branching scenario + quiz (not just click-next)
- Narration: Professional voice (not synthesized); conversational tone
```

## Calibration & Validation

Before deploying at scale:

1. **Calibrate the Rubric**: Run 5-10 diverse multimedia samples (videos, modules, slide decks) through your chosen model. Compare results to your expert judgment. Adjust dimension wording if scores are skewed.
2. **Test Edge Cases**: Validate against the 6 edge cases in `edge_cases_04.json`. All should score within your expected range.
4. **Document Threshold**: Define what score ranges mean (e.g., 4.5+ = "ready for launch", 3.5-4.4 = "revise", < 3.5 = "redesign").

## Troubleshooting

| Issue | Resolution |
|-------|-----------|
| Dimension scores vary widely between runs | Temperature may be too high. Use 0.0 for consistency. Ensure input descriptions are detailed and consistent. |
| Judge misses "Extraneous Load" issues | Be explicit about what's on-screen vs. narrated. Describe any background music, animations, or off-topic content. |
| Interactivity dimension is consistently low | Ensure you describe all interactions in detail. Generic descriptions like "interactive quiz" may not be specific enough; describe the question format, feedback type, etc. |
| Visual design dimension is hard to assess from text | Provide detailed descriptions: "Slide has 5 bullet points in small text on dark background with gold accent color." Screenshots help if possible. |
| Judge flags accessibility but you think it's fine | Run WCAG contrast checkers; ensure captions/transcripts exist; test with screen readers. Judge may be correctly identifying accessibility gaps. |
